# Oral Argument Preparation Protocol

A comprehensive oral argument preparation workflow. This skill produces a full hearing-prep memo the arguing attorney reads the night before and references at counsel table, plus a podium-ready quick reference card. The output is scripted where language matters and structured so nothing has to be re-read from the underlying briefing during the hearing.

## Step 1: Read All Briefing

Read every document provided or referenced — the motion, the response, the reply, and any exhibits. Do not skim. You must understand the full argument chain before producing anything.

If a case is named but no briefing is provided, request the documents before proceeding.

## Step 2: Produce the Prep Memo

**COMPREHENSIVENESS DIRECTIVE:** This memo is the hearing-prep document. The arguing attorney reads this the night before and references it at counsel table. Every section must have ENOUGH DETAIL that the attorney does not need to re-read the briefing. This means:
- Full legal reasoning, not summaries.
- Direct quotes from the filings where the critical language appears.
- Specific page numbers, paragraph numbers, and exhibit references.
- Opponent's specific cases named and distinguished — not "opponent argues X" but "opponent cites [Case A] and [Case B] for the proposition that..."
- Scripted language written as complete paragraphs the attorney can deliver verbatim, not one-liners.
- "If pressed" scripts that anticipate the SPECIFIC question the judge will ask based on the issues in play, with a full-paragraph answer.

**Do not abbreviate. Do not summarize. Write comprehensively.** A 10-15 page memo is expected. A 5-page memo is too short.

Structure the memo using EXACTLY these sections, in this order. Use Roman numerals for primary sections, capital letters for subsections, and lowercase letters or bullet points for sub-subsections.

---

### I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
- 5-7 bullet points, most important first. Each bullet should be a FULL SENTENCE with substance, not a fragment.
- Include overall strength assessment (X/10) with a one-sentence explanation.
- Identify the single most important thing the judge will focus on — explain WHY.
- Identify opponent's strongest play — and preview the counter in one sentence.
- Flag any threshold or procedural issues that may come first.
- Note any factual or procedural landmines (e.g., failure to depose, untimely filing, missing expert).

### II. CASE CLASSIFICATION
- A. Document type (what briefing was analyzed)
- B. Our role (Plaintiff / Defendant / Appellant / Appellee)
- C. Case posture (pre-trial, post-trial, appellate, etc.)
- D. Judge and court

### III. THRESHOLD / PROCEDURAL ISSUES
Address any procedural defenses or threshold issues the court may take up before reaching the merits. For each:
- a. The issue
- b. Opponent's position
- c. Your response (scripted)

If none, state "None identified" and move on.

### IV. OPENING STATEMENT — SCRIPTED (30 SECONDS)
Write a verbatim 30-second opening script following this structure:
1. Identify yourself and client
2. State what you need from the hearing
3. One sentence on why you win
4. Road map: "I intend to address [N] points, unless the Court has questions it would prefer to take up first."

The road map sentence invites the judge to redirect. Judges appreciate this.

### V. ARGUMENT-BY-ARGUMENT PREPARATION
For EACH distinct argument in the briefing, create a full subsection. This is the core of the memo — do NOT abbreviate. Each argument should be a comprehensive, deep-dive analysis, not a summary.

- **A. [Argument Name]**
  - a. Core argument — full legal reasoning in 3-5 sentences. Quote directly from the filing where the critical language appears. Name the specific page, paragraph, or finding that anchors the argument. Explain WHY the authority controls, not just THAT it exists.
  - b. Key authority — controlling case or statute with pincite AND a sentence explaining the holding in the context of this case.
  - c. Opponent's anticipated counter — what they will argue, with the SPECIFIC cases and authorities they cited. Name their cases. Do not generalize as "opponent will argue X" — state "Opponent cites [Case A], [Case B], and [Case C] for the proposition that..."
  - d. Your rebuttal — full paragraph. Explain why opponent's cases are distinguishable. Quote opponent's own language back to them where it helps.
  - e. Key distinction to drive home — the single most important point the judge needs to remember from this argument. Write it as a standalone sentence deliverable from memory.
  - f. "If the judge asks [specific question]" — write 2-3 specific questions the judge might ask about THIS argument (not generic questions — questions tied to the facts and authorities in play). For each, provide a verbatim scripted answer.

Case names must be italicized throughout. Every factual claim must be tied to a page number or paragraph in the briefing.

### VI. CASE LAW TABLE
List EVERY case cited or relevant to the oral argument. For each:

| Case | Citation | Proposition / Holding |
|---|---|---|
| *Case Name* | Full citation with pincite | One-sentence holding or the parenthetical you would use |

Order by: controlling authority first, then persuasive, then opponent's cases you must distinguish.

### VII. EXHIBIT INDEX
List EVERY exhibit referenced in the briefing. For each:

| Exhibit | Description | Referenced At | Cited In Support Of |
|---|---|---|---|
| Exhibit 1 | [What the document is] | Motion p. 4; Reply p. 7 | [What proposition it supports] |

If exhibits are not numbered in the briefing, assign provisional numbers and flag for confirmation.

### VIII. HOSTILE QUESTIONS — BENCH CARD
Generate 6-8 anticipated hostile questions with FULL scripted answers. Each answer should be a complete paragraph — not a one-liner. The answer should (1) address the question directly, (2) provide the legal or factual basis, (3) explain why the concern does not change the outcome, and (4) bridge back to the strongest argument if appropriate.

**Write questions the way THIS judge would phrase them** — based on the specific issues in the briefing, the opponent's arguments, and any procedural quirks of the case. Do not write generic questions like "Isn't this a weight issue?" unless that question genuinely arises from the briefing. Tie each question to a specific page, argument, or authority in the record.

**Framework for answering (standing instructions — do not include in output):**
1. Acknowledge the question — let it land. Brief pause.
2. Answer directly — do not pivot to your prepared argument.
3. Provide authority — record cite or case that supports your answer.
4. Bridge back if appropriate — connect to your broader argument.

**What NOT to do (standing instructions — do not include in output):**
- Do not say "with all due respect"
- Do not repeat the question
- Do not say "that's a great question"
- Do not change your position to please the judge unless you genuinely must concede
- Do not keep talking after the judge indicates they have heard enough

Format each Q&A pair as:
- **Q:** [Question in the judge's voice — specific to this case]
- **A:** [Full scripted answer — paragraph, not a sentence]

### IX. CONCESSION STRATEGY
Three categories:
- A. Concede freely — points that cost nothing and build credibility
- B. Concede if pressed — points that are not outcome-determinative
- C. NEVER concede — lines you hold no matter what

**Concession mechanics (standing instructions):**
"Your Honor, I will concede [the conceded point]. But that concession does not change the outcome here because [the dispositive point that survives the concession]."

Concede the minimum necessary. Redirect immediately. Do not dwell.

### X. FALLBACK POSITION
If the court signals it will rule against you on the primary ask:
- a. The fallback relief you request — be specific about what partial relief looks like.
- b. Verbatim script for pivoting to the fallback — write the exact words to say.
- c. Strategic note on why the fallback is still strong — explain how the fallback functionally achieves the same outcome or preserves the position.

### XI. RECALIBRATION SCENARIOS
For each scenario, one sentence on what to do:
- a. Court signals full denial
- b. Court signals full grant
- c. Court offers a middle ground / partial ruling
- d. Court asks you to address opponent's best argument before you planned to

**Recalibration signals to watch for (standing instructions):**
- Judge rules against you on a threshold point mid-argument
- Judge's questions consistently return to a weakness you hoped to minimize
- Judge appears to have already decided and is probing the scope of the ruling

**How to recalibrate:** "Your Honor, given the Court's concern about [the issue], let me address that directly before turning to [remaining arguments]."

### XII. REBUTTAL AMMUNITION
3-5 pre-written one-liners to hold in reserve for rebuttal. Rebuttal is a precision instrument, not a second opening.

**What belongs in rebuttal (standing instructions):**
- Direct response to the 1-2 most important points opponent just made
- Correction if opponent misstated the record or law
- Your single strongest argument if the judge seemed receptive

**What does NOT belong:**
- New arguments not raised in opening
- A recitation of everything you disagree with
- More than 2 minutes of content unless the court extends time

### XIII. CLOSING REQUEST — SCRIPTED
Verbatim closing script. End with a clear, specific request:
"For those reasons, Your Honor, we ask the Court to [specific relief]. We are prepared to [next step] at the Court's convenience."

### XIV. CANDID STRENGTH ASSESSMENT
For each argument, provide a strength rating (X/10) with:
- a. Rating
- b. Risk area (one sentence)
- c. Mitigation (one sentence)

Then an overall motion/hearing strength rating with commentary.

### XV. INDEPENDENT ASSESSMENT — WHAT YOU MAY HAVE MISSED

This section is UNCONSTRAINED. Do not treat it as a formality. Think independently. Answer each of these questions with substantive analysis, not a sentence of hand-waving:

- a. What is opposing counsel's most creative argument that has NOT appeared in the briefing?
- b. What would a law clerk flag for the judge if they researched this independently?
- c. What factual or legal weakness in your own position have you not yet confronted?
- d. Is there an argument or framing you have not considered that could change the outcome?
- e. What would you do differently if you were opposing counsel?
- f. Is there anything about this judge, this case posture, or this procedural moment that changes the standard playbook?

### XVI. CONFIDENCE ASSESSMENT (MANDATORY)

Before delivering output, categorize every substantive claim in your analysis:

**HIGH CONFIDENCE** (directly supported by record):
- [Statement] — Source: [page/exhibit/cite]

**MODERATE CONFIDENCE** (reasonable inference, not directly stated):
- [Inference] — Based on: [what I read] — Gap: [what would confirm this]

**LOW CONFIDENCE** (educated guess or assumption):
- [Assumption] — I assumed this because: [reason] — What I need: [specific fact/doc]

**INFORMATION I WISH I HAD:**
- [Specific document, fact, or context that would materially improve this analysis]

Rules: If a fact is missing and the analysis must work around it, state the assumption and flag it. Do NOT silently fill the gap with a guess.

### XVII. OPEN QUESTIONS
- Legal research verification tasks
- Time limit for argument (and which arguments to prioritize if short)
- Any information needed from the handling attorney before the hearing

### XVIII. READING THE BENCH — QUICK REFERENCE
Include this standing reference card at the end:
- Judge who writes while you speak = good sign; keep going
- Judge who stops writing and looks up = wants your attention; address directly
- Judge who interrupts early = has a specific concern; deal with it now
- Silence = do not fill it; let the judge think
- "I've read the briefs" = skip background, go to the dispositive issue
- "What's your strongest argument?" = answer that exact question first
- Multiple judges (appellate) = identify the swing vote; speak to them

## Step 3: Generate the Quick Reference Card

After producing the full memo, also generate a 3-page podium-ready quick reference card extracted from the full memo:

```
Page 1:
  BOTTOM LINE — one sentence why you win
  STRENGTH — X/10
  ASK — one sentence what you need
  OPENING SCRIPT — verbatim 30-second script
  ARGUMENTS — one line per argument + key case

Page 2:
  KEY AUTHORITIES — 6-8 cases, one-line holdings
  KEY EXHIBITS — exhibit #, description, page ref, what it proves
  OPPONENT'S BEST POINTS — their point → your one-line counter

Page 3:
  TOP 4 HOSTILE Q&A — short question, 2-sentence answer
  CONCESSIONS — freely (comma list) / never (comma list)
  FALLBACK — one sentence
  CLOSING SCRIPT — verbatim
```

Deliver both the full memo and the quick reference card as formatted Word documents (.docx) or PDFs. The quick reference card is the podium document — it should be the one that opens first.

## Standing Rules

- **Active voice in every sentence.** No passive constructions.
- **Case names italicized** throughout the memo and quick reference card.
- **Lettering and bullets for sub-points.** Primary sections use Roman numerals. Subsections use capital letters. Sub-subsections use lowercase letters or bullet points.
- **Scripted language in quotation marks.** Anything to be said verbatim goes in quotes.
- **No filler.** Every sentence earns its place. If a section has nothing to contribute, state "None identified" and move on.
